1. The Problem: Inference-Time Scaling

Proteina-Complexa (ICLR 2026 Oral) jointly co-designs protein structure and sequence via flow matching. At inference, test-time optimization (TTO) guides the denoising trajectory using AF2 rewards, steering toward binding-competent designs. The paper tests five inference-time optimization algorithms: Best-of-N, Beam Search, FK Steering, MCTS, and Generate & Hallucinate.

Figure 7: Scaling Analysis from the Paper

Figure 7 plots average unique successes vs. GPU hours for 12 easy and 7 hard targets:

Easy Targets (12)

Best-of-N achieves the most unique successes, outperforming Beam Search. Both continue scaling across the measured range (2.5–12.5 GPU hours). FK Steering ranks third. The model already produces good binders for these targets; more independent samples help most.

Hard Targets (7)

Beam Search achieves the most unique successes and maintains a roughly linear scaling slope. Best-of-N grows sub-linearly, with the gap widening at higher compute budgets. FK Steering ranks second. MCTS performs comparably to Best-of-N. Generate & Hallucinate is worst.

Goal: Build a system where inference-time scaling doesn't plateau on hard targets — analogous to how o1/o3 achieve better results with more test-time compute in LLMs. The agent reads structured intermediate signals and adaptively allocates compute across the full action space, rather than using a fixed strategy for all targets.

What the paper leaves open: The ICLR paper tests each algorithm with fixed default parameters. The companion validation paper (Latent Generative Search) tests 4 sampling noise combinations in aggregate across 75 solvable targets (Table 2, Sec B.4) and uses multi-objective rewards (iPAE, H-bonds, RF3, geometric constraints) for the 127-target campaign. However, neither paper explores per-target parameter tuning, systematic per-difficulty noise analysis, or adaptive multi-round strategies. Our project maps this larger action space with per-target pass rate analysis.

2. The Pipeline & Action Space

How the Pipeline Works

Proteina-Complexa generates proteins through a 4-stage pipeline. The model jointly co-designs backbone structure (bb_ca) and residue-level sequence (local_latents) via coupled flow matching. No external inverse folding is needed for the "self" sequence — it is decoded from the latent space.

Generate: t=0 (noise) ──denoise 400 steps──▶ t=400 (structure + sequence) │ Beam Search: ├─ checkpoint 0: branch x n_branch │ rollout to step 400 ──▶ AF2 score │ keep top beam_width ├─ checkpoint 1: branch ──▶ score ──▶ prune ├─ ... └─ final checkpoint: output PDBs with co-designed sequences │ Filter: rank by AF2 iPAE, deduplicate sequences │ Evaluate: AF2 refold (self seq) + ProteinMPNN redesign + refold (mpnn seq) │ Analyze: Success = iPAE*31 ≤ 7 AND pLDDT ≥ 0.9 AND scRMSD < 1.5A Diversity = FoldSeek structural clustering

"self" = sequence decoded from the model's local_latents (co-designed with structure). "mpnn" = ProteinMPNN redesigns the same backbone from scratch (post-hoc, independent of the model). Both are refolded with AF2 to check designability.

FK Steering: A Continuous Explore-Exploit Spectrum

FK Steering is identical to Beam Search except at the selection step: instead of hard top-k, it samples with P(beam) ~ exp(reward / temperature).

  • T → 0: Converges to Beam Search (greedy exploitation)
  • T → ∞: Converges to Best-of-N (pure exploration)
  • Intermediate T: Soft exploration-exploitation tradeoff

In Figure 7, FK Steering with default T=0.1 already ranks 2nd on hard targets (above Best-of-N) and 3rd on easy targets. The key question is whether tuning T per target can close the gap with Beam Search on hard targets while matching Best-of-N on easy targets.

5. Key Findings

1. No universal winner across targets

Wide beam wins on PD1, PDL1, TrkA, CrSAS6, DerF7. Deep beam wins on Claudin1, SpCas9, CD45, HER2_AAV, BetV1. The Autoresearch sweep confirms: the optimal config changes for 4/10 proteins when switching from top-1 to top-16 selection. This target-dependence is the core motivation for adaptive routing.

2. Beam search dramatically outperforms random sampling

With all evaluation pass rates complete: IFNAR2 jumps from 24% (notto) to 100% (deep); PD1 from 8.5% to 100%; Claudin1 from 0.5% to 100%; CrSAS6 from 4% to 100%. Even CD45 (hard) goes from 0% to 25%. All 3 hard targets (CD45, HER2_AAV, BetV1) score 0% with notto but achieve non-zero pass rates with beam search. Mean iPAE drops 3-5x across all targets.

3. Co-designed sequences match or outperform ProteinMPNN in 10/11 targets

Considering the best config per target, self pass rate ≥ mpnn pass rate in 10 of 11 targets. The exception is HER2_AAV-deep (self 3.1% vs mpnn 31.3%), where the model generates good backbones but suboptimal sequences. This anomaly suggests HER2_AAV may benefit from targeted sequence refinement.

4. Hard targets remain unsolved

BetV1: 18.8% self pass rate (deep), 0% mpnn. HER2_AAV: 3.1% self (deep). CD45: 25% self (deep). BetV1-wide produced only 2 samples (timeout). These targets likely need qualitatively different strategies: different reward signals, longer binders, different hotspot selections, or stronger exploration mechanisms.

5. Large portions of the action space remain unexplored

The ICLR paper tested all 5 algorithms with fixed default parameters (η=0.1, N=4, L=4, K=100). The companion paper (Latent Generative Search) tested 4 sampling noise combinations in aggregate across 75 solvable targets and used multi-objective rewards for its 127-target campaign. However, the per-target breakdown of what noise level helps hard targets specifically vs. easy ones, systematic parameter tuning within algorithms (e.g., FK Steering temperature, MCTS exploration constant), and adaptive multi-round routing — none of these have been explored. Our project maps this space with per-target analysis and builds an adaptive routing agent on top of it.

  • Conclusion: An agentic system that dynamically selects the right config per target will outperform any fixed config. The matrix is now complete — ready to build the router.

5b. Deep Dive: What Do Noise & Hallucination Actually Do?

Our two most impactful interventions — increased sampling noise and sequence hallucination — operate on fundamentally different parts of the generation pipeline. Understanding why each works for specific targets (and fails for others) is the foundation of our agentic routing logic.

What is sc_scale_noise?

Proteina-Complexa generates proteins by solving a stochastic differential equation (SDE) that transforms Gaussian noise into structure + sequence over 400 denoising steps. The sc_scale_noise parameter (η in the paper) controls how much random noise is injected at each step:

delta_x = (velocity + gt * score) * dt + sqrt(2 * gt * sc_scale_noise * dt) * random_noise
  • η = 0: Pure ODE. Deterministic — same initial noise always produces the same protein.
  • η = 0.1 (default): Low-temperature SDE. Tight, consistent structures. The paper’s default for all experiments.
  • η = 0.2: Higher stochasticity. More structurally diverse backbones. Our experiments show this doubles CD45 self pass rate (25%→47%) by exploring a wider region of fold space near the binding interface.

Two independent controls: bb_ca.sc_scale_noise (backbone Cα noise) and local_latents.sc_scale_noise (sequence/sidechain noise) can be set independently. Our Exp 6 and 12 show that both must be raised together to 0.2 for the effect — raising only backbone (bb=0.2, lat=0.1) gives 9.4% (worse than default), and raising only latent (bb=0.1, lat=0.4) gives 0% (catastrophic).

What is Sequence Hallucination?

Sequence hallucination is a post-generation refinement step that optimizes the co-designed sequence using AlphaFold2 as a differentiable oracle. After the flow model produces a binder (structure + sequence), hallucination iteratively modifies the sequence to minimize AF2’s predicted interface error, in three stages:

StageWhat HappensIterations
Softmax optimization AF2 refolds the complex while amino acid probabilities are represented as continuous softmax vectors. Temperature anneals from soft to hard, gradually committing to specific residues. Backpropagation through AF2 updates the sequence probabilities. 45 iters
One-hot optimization Switch to discrete one-hot amino acid representations. AF2 still provides gradients but through a straight-through estimator. 5 iters
Greedy mutation Try single-residue mutations and keep those that improve AF2 scores (PSSM semigreedy). No gradients — pure forward-pass evaluation. 15 iters

Key insight: Hallucination does not change the backbone — it only rewrites the amino acid sequence to better match the fixed 3D structure according to AF2. This is why it works spectacularly for HER2_AAV (where the backbone is already good but the co-designed sequence is poor) and fails for CD45 (where the backbone itself needs improvement).

Note: The ICLR paper’s “Generate & Hallucinate” uses only Stage 3 (greedy mutation), which is fast but limited. Our experiments use all three stages (softmax + one-hot + greedy), which is slower but more powerful for hard targets.

Exp 7 Detail: CFG Guidance Scale (Failed)

8 jobs attempted. Only guidance_w=1.0 (control) ran successfully. All guidance_w > 1.0 jobs crashed with: AssertionError: Classifier-free guidance should only be turned on for conditional generation.

Post-Mortem: Why CFG Failed

  • Root cause: The codebase requires fold_cond=True (CATH code conditioning) for CFG to activate. Binder design doesn’t use CATH conditioning — it conditions on target structure directly. The unconditional forward pass (needed for CFG) isn’t available in binder mode.
  • Technical detail: CFG computes v_guided = w*v_cond + (1-w)*v_uncond. The model needs to produce both conditional and unconditional predictions. For binders, the model is always conditioned on the target — there’s no “unconditional” mode to subtract.
  • Alternative: Autoguidance (ag_ratio=1.0) uses a separate “bad model” checkpoint instead of unconditional predictions, but requires training a degraded model. Not currently available.
  • Conclusion: CFG guidance amplification is not applicable to binder design without model modifications. The two remaining paths to improve backbone quality are: (a) sc_scale_noise tuning (Exp 2, confirmed effective), and (b) LoRA finetuning / trajectory recycling (Exp 10–11, planned).

Risk: Too high noise may break the model's learned priors, producing clash-ridden or unphysical structures. 0.4 may already be too high.

Cost: ~8 GPU-hours

Exp 3 Composite Reward Signals

Hypothesis: Adding secondary reward signals to beam search pruning improves the quality of surviving beams, particularly for hard targets where iPAE-only pruning may discard structurally viable candidates.

Motivation: Current TTO uses only AF2 iPAE for pruning. The codebase has 4 reward models: AF2 (17 metrics), RF3 (14 metrics), TMOL (H-bonds, electrostatics), and Bioinformatics (shape complementarity, dSASA). TMOL and Bioinformatics are fast (no neural network inference) and could add signal at negligible cost.

Setup:

  • 2 hard targets: CD45 (25% deep pass rate), BetV1 (18.8% deep pass rate)
  • 3 reward compositions:
    (a) iPAE only (baseline, weight -1.0)
    (b) iPAE (-1.0) + pLDDT (0.3) + i_ptm (-0.2) [multi-AF2-metric]
    (c) iPAE (-1.0) + i_ptm (-0.2) + tmol H-bond (0.1) [cross-model composite]
  • Fixed: deep config (beam_width=8, n_branch=3)
  • Total: 6 jobs

Hydra overrides (option b):

complexa design configs/search_binder_local_pipeline.yaml \
  ++generation.reward_model.reward_models.af2folding.reward_weights.plddt=0.3 \
  ++generation.reward_model.reward_models.af2folding.reward_weights.i_ptm=-0.2

Success criteria: Does any composite reward achieve higher pass rate than iPAE-only on hard targets?

Risk: Additional reward signals may conflict with iPAE, causing the beam to optimize for the wrong objective. Need to verify TMOL/bioinformatics actually have non-zero variance on Proteina-generated backbones (prior work found clash and RoG had zero variance).

Cost: ~6 GPU-hours

Exp 4 Sequence Refinement for High-Backbone / Low-Sequence Targets

Hypothesis: For targets where the model generates good backbones but weak sequences (self_pass << mpnn_pass or backbone iPAE is good but self scRMSD is poor), AF2-guided sequence hallucination can rescue designs.

Motivation: HER2_AAV-deep shows self 3.1% but mpnn 31.3% — the backbones are viable but the co-designed sequences are suboptimal. The codebase has a sequence_hallucination refinement stage that optimizes sequences via AF2 gradients.

Important context: In Figure 7, "Generate & Hallucinate" (which uses this refinement) performed worst among all 5 algorithms. However, that applied hallucination to all designs. Our hypothesis is that applying it selectively — only to designs with good backbones but poor sequences — could be beneficial.

Setup:

  • Take top-16 designs from HER2_AAV-deep (clear backbone > sequence gap)
  • Also top-16 from PDL1-deep (100% self, 37.5% mpnn — moderate gap)
  • Apply sequence hallucination: 45 softmax iters + 15 greedy iters with default loss weights
  • Re-evaluate with AF2 refolding

Hydra overrides:

complexa design configs/search_binder_local_pipeline.yaml \
  ++generation.refinement.algorithm=sequence_hallucination \
  ++generation.refinement.enable_soft_optimization=true \
  ++generation.refinement.enable_greedy_optimization=true

Success criteria: Does refinement increase self pass rate on HER2_AAV from 3.1% toward mpnn's 31.3%?

Cost: ~4 GPU-hours

Exp 7 Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) Scale Sweep

Hypothesis: Increasing guidance_w above 1.0 amplifies the conditional signal (target-conditioned velocity field minus unconditional prior), pushing the denoising trajectory harder toward the target binding interface. This is analogous to high CFG scales in text-to-image diffusion models and could dramatically improve backbone quality for hard targets.

Motivation: This is the single most impactful unexplored knob. The default guidance_w=1.0 means CFG is completely disabled — v_guided = 1.0 * v_cond + 0.0 * v_uncond = v_cond. With guidance_w > 1.0, the formula becomes v_guided = w * v_cond + (1-w) * v_uncond, which subtracts the unconditional prior and amplifies the target-specific signal. In image generation, CFG scales of 2–7 are standard and transformative for quality. No prior Proteina experiment has tested this.

Setup:

  • 2 targets: CD45 (hard, 25% deep baseline), BetV1 (hard, 18.8% deep baseline)
  • 4 guidance weights: 1.0 (control), 1.5, 2.0, 3.0
  • Fixed: deep config (beam_width=8, n_branch=3)
  • Total: 8 jobs (4 weights × 2 targets)

Hydra overrides:

complexa design configs/search_binder_local_pipeline.yaml \
  ++generation.args.guidance_w=2.0

Risk: Very high guidance_w (3.0+) may cause mode collapse or degenerate structures, similar to how high CFG in image models produces oversaturated images. Also requires the model to have been trained with unconditional dropout — need to verify.

Success criteria: Does guidance_w > 1.0 increase self pass rate on CD45 above the 25% beam baseline?

Cost: ~8 GPU-hours

Phase 1.5: Improve Generation Quality (Engineering Required)

These experiments go beyond tuning existing hyperparameters — they modify the model or the inference procedure itself. Higher engineering cost, but potentially the highest ceiling for hard targets.

Exp 10 LoRA Finetuning on Successful Binders

Hypothesis: The Complexa denoiser was pre-trained on general protein structures. Finetuning with LoRA adapters on successful binder designs shifts the model’s prior distribution toward structures that actually bind, improving backbone quality at generation time.

Motivation: For CD45, only 25% of beam-selected backbones pass even with deep search. The model’s prior simply doesn't cover the right conformational space for hard interfaces. Rather than searching harder in a bad prior (Exp 1–7), we shift the prior itself.

Two approaches:

  • Option A — Supervised: Finetune on all ~500+ successful designs across all 11 targets. The model learns “what successful binders look like” in general. Risk: dominated by easy targets (IFNAR2 has 100% pass rate, contributing most training data).
  • Option B — DPO-style: Train on (successful backbone, failed backbone) pairs from the same target. The model learns to distinguish what makes a binder succeed vs. fail for a given interface type. More data-efficient for hard targets (CD45: 8 positive + 24 negative pairs from deep). Conceptually analogous to RLHF/DPO for LLMs — aligning the model toward reward-preferred outputs.

Setup:

  • Collect all successful/failed backbone structures from Phase 0 + Phase 1 runs
  • Add LoRA adapters (rank 16–32) to the Complexa denoiser transformer blocks
  • Train for 500–2000 steps with small learning rate (1e-5)
  • Evaluate: run deep beam search on CD45 and BetV1 with finetuned model

Engineering required: LoRA wrapper around Complexa denoiser, denoising score matching loss on successful structures (Option A) or DPO preference loss (Option B).

Risk: Overfitting — only ~500 total successes across all targets, and only 8 for CD45. Mitigation: low LoRA rank, early stopping on held-out target, regularization to base weights.

Cost: ~20 GPU-hours training + ~10 GPU-hours evaluation. ~1-2 days engineering.

Exp 11 Trajectory Recycling / Warm-Starting

Hypothesis: Successful designs follow “good paths” through the denoising trajectory. By saving intermediate latent states from successful trajectories and re-starting new beam searches from those states (with small noise perturbations), we can do local search in trajectory space to find more successes without re-exploring the global fold topology.

Motivation: The Complexa denoiser runs 400 steps. The first ~130 steps determine the global backbone fold topology. For CD45's 8 successful designs, steps 0–130 found the right fold geometry. By seeding new runs from t=130 of these trajectories + noise, we skip the hardest part (finding the right fold) and explore variations of the interface geometry.

Setup:

  • Phase A: Modify beam search to save intermediate latent states at each checkpoint
  • Phase B: Run deep beam search on CD45, collect latent states at t=130 from the 8 successful trajectories
  • Phase C: Start 8 new beam searches, each seeded from one successful t=130 state + Gaussian noise (σ=0.05–0.2)
  • Compare: unique successes from warm-started runs vs. cold-started runs with same GPU budget

Engineering required: Modify search/beam_search.py to save/load intermediate trajectory states. Add noise perturbation to loaded states. ~1 day engineering.

Risk: If the 8 successful trajectories share a single mode (all converged to the same backbone topology), warm-starting will produce near-duplicates rather than diverse new designs. Check with FoldSeek clustering first.

Cost: ~8 GPU-hours runs + ~1 day engineering

Exp 5 Diagnostic Signal Extraction

Hypothesis: A cheap single-pass diagnostic (N=32, ~5 min generation + ~20 min AF2 eval) contains enough signal to predict target difficulty and route to the optimal algorithm/config.

Setup:

  • For all 11 targets, extract from notto runs (N=200): mean iPAE, iPAE variance, % below 0.226 threshold, iPAE distribution skewness, mean pLDDT, mean RMSD, sequence entropy
  • Correlate each signal with: (a) optimal config (deep vs wide), (b) self pass rate, (c) difficulty tier
  • Test whether a simple threshold rule (e.g., "if mean_iPAE > 0.7, use deep") achieves >80% routing accuracy

Expected output: A decision tree mapping diagnostic statistics to recommended config.

Cost: Analysis only — all 11 notto runs complete with evaluation data

Exp 6 LLM Routing Validation

Hypothesis: An LLM reading structured JSON (diagnostic statistics + target metadata) can route targets to configs more accurately than simple threshold rules, especially for medium-difficulty targets where the optimal config is ambiguous.

Setup:

  • Prepare structured JSON per target: iPAE distribution (mean, std, min, max, quartiles), pLDDT distribution, notto pass rate, target metadata (length, hotspot count, interface type)
  • Prompt: "Given these diagnostic statistics, recommend: algorithm (beam-search / fk-steering), beam_width, n_branch, checkpoint schedule, and explain your reasoning"
  • Compare LLM routing vs. threshold rules vs. oracle (actual best config from sweep)
  • Leave-one-out: train on 10 targets, predict 11th

Key question: Does the LLM's ability to reason about biological context (e.g., "this target has a large flat interface, which typically benefits from wider beam search") add value beyond statistical pattern matching?

Cost: API calls only

Exp 8 Multi-Round Adaptive Pipeline

Hypothesis: An agent that runs diagnostic Round 0, selects config for Round 1, reads results and adjusts for Round 2 produces more unique successes per GPU-hour than any fixed strategy.

Setup:

  • 3 targets: IFNAR2 (easy), SpCas9 (medium), CD45 (hard)
  • Agent protocol:
    Round 0: single-pass N=32 diagnostic (~0.5 GPU-hr)
    Round 1: agent-selected config based on diagnostic (~1 GPU-hr)
    Round 2: agent adjusts based on Round 1 results (~1 GPU-hr)
    Round 3: final run with refined config (~1 GPU-hr)
  • Compare total unique successes after 3.5 GPU-hours vs:
    (a) fixed deep config x3 rounds = 3 GPU-hours
    (b) fixed wide config x3 rounds = 3 GPU-hours
    (c) oracle (always use best config) x3 rounds = 3 GPU-hours

Agent decisions between rounds: Adjust beam_width, n_branch, FK temperature, nsamples, sc_scale_noise, guidance_w, reward composition. Record all decisions and reasoning for analysis.

Success criteria: Agent achieves ≥80% of oracle performance on at least 2/3 targets.

Cost: ~15 GPU-hours (3.5h x 3 targets + baselines)

Open questions:
(1) Does FK Steering temperature tuning improve over Beam Search? Answered (Exp 1): No. Beam dominates; FK monotonically worse.
(2) Does CFG guidance_w > 1.0 improve backbone quality? Answered (Exp 7): Not applicable. CFG requires CATH fold conditioning, N/A for binders.
(3) Do composite rewards improve beam pruning? Answered (Exp 3): No. Heavy interface weights kill generation (0/32). Moderate weights are neutral.
(4) Does sc_scale_noise > 0.1 significantly improve hard targets? Answered (Exp 2+5): Yes. noise=0.20 gives 47% vs 25% (p=0.003, N=96). Perfectly reproducible across 3 runs.
(5) Does backbone vs. sequence noise matter independently? Answered (Exp 6): Backbone noise is the driver. lat=0.2 alone: no effect (p=1.0). lat=0.4 alone: catastrophic (0%, p=0.005). New mechanistic insight.
(6) Can sequence hallucination rescue weak sequences? Answered (Exp 4+4b): Yes for HER2_AAV (p<10⁻⁶). Paired within-run control confirms causal.
(7) Does hallucination generalize to other hard targets (BetV1)? Answered (Exp 9): Weakly. Self: 18.8%→25.0% (n.s.), but MPNN rescued 0%→9.4%. Effect is target-dependent.
(8) Can noise=0.2 + hallucination jointly boost CD45? Answered (Exp 8): No — negative interaction. Halluc hurts at high noise (46.9%→18.8%, p=0.032). Interventions are mutually exclusive.
(9) Does noise=0.20 help or hurt HER2_AAV and BetV1? Exp 10b/10c queued.
(10) What is the MPNN-preserving sweet spot for noise? Exp 12a (noise=0.15) queued.
(11) Is bb noise alone sufficient (bb=0.2, lat=0.1)? Exp 12b queued.
(12) Does the noise+halluc negative interaction replicate on other targets? Exp 13a/13b queued.
(13) Do hallucination and baseline effects replicate at N=96? Exp 11a-j queued (10 scale-up runs).
(14) Core agentic question: No single config is optimal for all targets. Can a diagnostic round (cheap N=32) identify which config to use? (Phase 2b, planned.)
