LLM-Guided Inference-Time Scaling for De Novo Protein Design

Minkyu Jeon  ·  Princeton University Updated: April 2026 In Progress
Central Thesis

Current de novo protein design pipelines use fixed hyperparameter strategies applied uniformly to all targets. We hypothesize that an LLM reading structured intermediate signals from the generative trajectory can adaptively route compute — matching Best-of-N to easy targets and beam search to hard targets — enabling inference-time scaling that does not plateau as GPU hours increase.

Part I Background & Motivation

Background

De novo protein binder design — computationally generating proteins that bind to a target of interest — is a central challenge in drug discovery, synthetic biology, and therapeutic development. Recent generative models like Proteina-Complexa (ICLR 2026 Oral) have dramatically improved in-silico success rates by coupling flow-matching backbone generation with test-time compute optimization (TTO).

Proteina-Complexa jointly co-designs protein structure and sequence in a single generative process using two coupled latents: backbone Cα coordinates (bb_ca) and residue-level sequence latents (local_latents). At inference time, beam search guides the denoising trajectory using intermediate AF2 rewards, steering the model toward binding-competent solutions.

Motivation

[Figure 7 from Proteina-Complexa paper — load locally to view]
Figure 7 (Proteina-Complexa, ICLR 2026): Inference-time scaling across 12 easy and 7 hard protein targets. On easy targets, Best-of-N (green) outperforms beam search (red). On hard targets, beam search keeps scaling while Best-of-N plateaus around 15 GPU hours.

Figure 7 from the paper reveals a clean empirical pattern:

  • Easy targets: Best-of-N (random sampling with large N) beats beam search. Both keep climbing — the success basin is large enough for random exploration to work efficiently.
  • Hard targets: Best-of-N plateaus around 15 GPU hours. Beam search continues scaling. The success basin is tiny — random sampling almost never lands there.
The Gap: No existing system automatically detects whether a target is easy or hard at the start of a campaign, or adapts its search strategy in response to intermediate signals. Every campaign uses the same fixed configuration regardless of what the trajectory reveals.

Goal & Scientific Claim

We are building a system with a falsifiable claim:

Falsifiable Claim

An LLM that reads structured intermediate signals from a generative design trajectory can classify target difficulty before exhausting the compute budget, and route accordingly — achieving inference-time scaling that does not plateau on hard targets while remaining compute-efficient on easy targets.

How TTO Works: Beam Search over the Joint Latent State

The flow matching model denoises two coupled latents simultaneously, starting from pure Gaussian noise at t=1.0 and converging to a clean sample at t=0:

  • bb_ca — backbone Cα coordinates (structure)
  • local_latents — compressed amino acid identity via a learned autoencoder (sequence)

Beam search prunes the joint (structure, sequence) latent state at intermediate checkpoints using AF2 multimer as the reward model: 

  t=0 (noise)
    ↓ denoise to step 65
    branch each beam × n_branch → lookahead to completion → AF2 score
    keep top beam_width by i_PAE
    ↓ denoise to step 130
    branch → score → prune
    ... (6 pruning steps for "deep" config) ...
    ↓ step 400: final structures with co-designed sequences

Three algorithms are available:

AlgorithmDescriptionBest for
single-pass (Best-of-N)N independent random trajectories, no reward guidance during generationEasy targets
beam-searchGuided search: branch → AF2 → prune at each checkpointHard targets
best-of-nN×replicas, keep best replica per seed at each checkpointModerate

Sequence Evaluation: Self vs MPNN

After generation, each structure is evaluated under two sequence types:

  • self: The sequence co-designed by Proteina (decoded from local_latents)
  • mpnn: Alternative sequences generated by ProteinMPNN redesigning the same backbone from scratch
Key insight: In our current experiments, self success rates are often higher than or equal to MPNN redesigns, suggesting Proteina's joint co-design is already producing competitive sequences — not just good backbones.

Proposed LLM Routing Architecture

  Step 0–65: run cheap pilot (all beams, small n_branch)
       ↓
  Extract structured JSON at step 65:
  {
    "reward_improvement_rate": 0.06,
    "beam_diversity_rmsd": 3.2,
    "top1_reward": -0.31,
    "convergence_flag": false,
    "historical_context": {
      "easy_target_step65_mean": -0.19,
      "hard_target_step65_mean": -0.44
    }
  }
       ↓
  LLM classifies difficulty + recommends config:
    "Easy"  → Best-of-N (no more expensive AF2 calls)
    "Medium" → deep/frequent pruning (E_fine_selector)
    "Hard"  → wide beam (C_deep_exploiter)
       ↓
  Route remaining compute accordingly

Critically, the LLM does not read protein structures or sequences directly — it reads computed statistics from analysis code it also helps write. This is the SCORE-style approach: LLM writes analysis code → code produces structured JSON → LLM reasons over numbers.

Key Findings So Far

Finding 1 — No universal winner: Wide beats deep on PD1, PDL1, CrSAS6, DerF7. Deep beats wide on CD45, Claudin1. This confirms target-specific optimal configs exist — the core motivation for adaptive routing.
Finding 2 — Self sequences are competitive: In 8/8 completed targets, Proteina's co-designed sequences pass at the same rate or better than MPNN redesigns across at least one config. The lone exception is Claudin1-wide (self=7 vs mpnn=10), but Claudin1-deep reverses this (self=32 vs mpnn=12). Joint co-design is not just producing good backbones — it's producing competitive sequences too.
Pending — Critical data point: No-TTO N=200 results will complete ~2.5h after beam search jobs. These baselines are needed to reproduce Figure 7 locally and confirm easy/hard × algorithm interaction.
Caution — Self success rates: Several easy targets show 32/32 self successes with beam search. This may indicate the success threshold is too permissive for these targets, or that these targets are genuinely easy for Proteina. Cross-checking against no-TTO will clarify.

Future Directions

Conformation-Aware Design (Longer Term)

BioEmu-generated conformational ensembles of target proteins can seed a Target State Bank. Hard targets may benefit from designing against multiple conformations — testing whether the binder works across the target's natural flexibility. We have a working BioEmu pipeline on PD-L1 (99 conformations, mean pairwise RMSD = 1.74Å). IFNAR2 is the next candidate (known domain motion, PDB 2lag) — likely to show richer conformational heterogeneity.

Generalization Beyond Proteina-Complexa

The LLM routing framework is model-agnostic. Any generative model that produces intermediate scored samples can feed this pipeline. The abstraction: intermediate oracle score → structured JSON → difficulty classification → hyperparameter routing. Proteina-Complexa is the first implementation; RFdiffusion and future models are natural next targets.

The Publishable Claim

Target claim: A conformation-aware, LLM-guided adaptive inference pipeline achieves inference-time scaling that does not plateau on hard protein targets — outperforming fixed Best-of-N and fixed beam search under matched compute budgets, across both protein and ligand binder design.
